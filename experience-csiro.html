CSIRO | Nov 2025 - Present | Kensington, WA

Executive Summary (TL;DR)

Developed a physics-inspired forecasting engine (PID-Net) that predicts radio interference 12 hours in advance, preventing data loss and optimizing scheduling for Australia’s $1B+ telescope infrastructure.

The Bigger Context: CSIRO and Australia's Radio Telescopes

CSIRO operates and supports multiple world-class radio astronomy facilities across Australia. These include ASKAP, Murriyang (the Parkes Radio Telescope), the Australia Telescope Compact Array (ATCA), the Canberra Deep Space Communication Complex (CDSCC), and the upcoming SKA-Low. Together, they enable frontier science while demanding highly reliable, 24/7 operations.

My Focus: ASKAP

My work focused on ASKAP. It is located inside a radio quiet zone to minimize interference from urban radio-frequency sources. In practice, however, atmospheric ducting can still carry distant radio-frequency interference (RFI) over long ranges into the telescope's observing band.

The Problem: Atmospheric ‘Tunnels’ for Noise

Under normal conditions, Earth's curvature naturally blocks distant terrestrial radio signals from reaching the observatory. However, certain weather conditions can cause a temperature inversion in the lower atmosphere, forming a refractive "duct".

Rather than escaping into space, radio signals from coastal cities or maritime vessels get trapped in this atmospheric layer. The duct guides these unwanted signals hundreds of kilometers over the horizon directly into the "Radio Quiet Zone" where ASKAP resides.

Why This Matters Operationally

The High Stakes of Observing Time: Astronomers can't simply turn on the telescope whenever they want. Observing time on ASKAP is highly competitive; scientists must submit proposals months in advance to a Time Allocation Committee. If their hard-won scheduled time slot unfortunately coincides with a severe ducting event, their observations can be entirely ruined by RFI.

Furthermore, ASKAP generates data at an enormous rate (~2.5 GB per second). Raw observations are sent through Pawsey supercomputing infrastructure for preprocessing before astronomers can use them. If strong interference is present, that data can still consume compute and storage resources first, only to be flagged or discarded later.

This creates a real cost in missed scientific opportunities, infrastructure load, and wasted observing value. Since these facilities operate continuously, the goal is to dynamically schedule observations that are more robust to RFI and reserve critical observations for cleaner windows.

What I Built

I developed a predictive ducting-risk workflow for ASKAP that shifts planning from reactive response to proactive scheduling. The system provides a 12-hour rolling forecast with 1-hour resolution, offering guidance early enough to inform day-to-day observing decisions and make better use of telescope and HPC resources.

After rigorous testing on two-and-a-half years of real observatory data, the model now delivers up to 12 hours of advance warning with solid accuracy. Even at the longest horizon, it still correctly flags the majority of ducting events. By analyzing the model's attention weights, we found it implicitly captured the underlying physics, automatically recognizing that ducting begins at specific frequencies and spreads in a predictable 'cascade.' Furthermore, it identified ocean winds and overnight cooling as the primary meteorological triggers.

Best of all, the entire model is surprisingly lightweight (under 250,000 parameters). This allows it to run in real time on ordinary hardware right at the observatory, with no supercomputer required.

Practical Outcome

  • Better observation planning under ducting risk instead of post-event damage control.
  • Lower waste of compute and analyst time on heavily corrupted data streams.
  • Higher return on expensive 24/7 observing time through risk-aware allocation.
  • Support for mission-critical operations by integrating forecasts into real decision workflows.
